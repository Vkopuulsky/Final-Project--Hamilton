John Laurens

John Laurens was an American soldier and a statesman from South Carolina. He was ranked Lieutenant colonel and fought in the Battle of Brandywine, Battle of Germantown, Battle of Monmouth, Battle of Coosawhatchie, Seige of Savannah, Siege of Yorktown, Battle of the Combahee River.

After their mother died, John and his brothers were sent to Geneva, Switzerland for their education. It was there he was introduced to the libertarian ideals he later kept about slavery. The Laurens boys then traveled to England to study law. Common Sense, the pamphlet by Thomas Paine, that was wildly circulating the colonies sparked his interest in the war effort at home. He wrote to his father asking to return home, and in spite of his father's refusal, John determined to return.

On August 27, 1782, Laurens was riding at the front of his troops, ordered to maintain a defensive position to stop British foraging parties along the Combanhee River. He was ambushed, and, with his customary courage and recklessness, he chose to attack back rather than retreat. On the second British volley, he was struck and fell from his horse, dying on the field. He was buried the following day on a nearby plantation.
